Stroy

Shuddhi Ayodhya (Ananthika Sanilkumar) is a young writer and martial artist raised by her mother in Ooty after the death of her army father.
With clarity, courage and creative talent, she pours her grief into writing and emerges as a rising literary voice.
When Varun (Hanu Reddy) walks into her life, love briefly blooms before heartbreak sets her on a path of self-reinvention.
As Shuddhi moves through these emotional seasons, the film explores how each “spring” transforms her.

Review

8 Vasantalu is an ambitious and visually rich film that opens on a strong note. Director Phanindra Narsetti crafts a story that leans into emotion, reflection and visual poetry. With meaningful friendships, an unwavering mother, and moments of inner conflict, the film builds a layered narrative around loss, identity and resilience.
Ananthika Sanilkumar delivers a quietly compelling performance, capturing both the strength and stillness of Shuddhi with authenticity. Hanu Reddy brings charm to Varun, while Ravi Duggirala, as Sanjay, leaves a gentle impact in the final act. The supporting cast, including Kanna Pasunoori and Sanjana Hardageri, provide warmth and grounding.
The first half flows like lyrical prose, beautifully supported by Hesham Abdul Wahab’s score and poetic visuals by Vishwanath Reddy, which capture the serenity of Ooty, the calm of Kashmir and the spirit of Varanasi. Despite its elegant start, the film struggles in its second half. The narrative begins to feel repetitive, and the writing leans heavily on literary and philosophical musings. Scenes linger without adding much, and the introspection begins to weigh down the pace. Dialogue that initially feels profound starts to sound overstated.
There are still moments that resonate. A mother’s explanation of generational dreams and a poet’s reflections on personal identity are thoughtful and sincere. The emotional tone remains intact, even when the storytelling becomes indulgent.
Technically, the film holds up well. The cinematography and sound design work in tandem to create mood and atmosphere. The action scenes, particularly one in Varanasi, are crisp and visually satisfying. However, a tighter edit in the second half would have helped retain the emotional momentum.
8 Vasantalu is not fast-paced or conventional. It is poetic, meditative and emotionally layered. While the second half slows down and leans into overstatement, it still offers moments worth holding on to for those who appreciate poetic storytelling, visual depth and a strong female lead.
